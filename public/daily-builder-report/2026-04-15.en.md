## Tech Radar

### Did any major company shut down or downgrade a product?

**Backblaze silently downgraded** its personal backup product by excluding OneDrive, Dropbox, and potentially other cloud-synced folders. The article reveals this wasn't announced — users discovered it when restores failed. The [original blog post](https://rareese.com/posts/backblaze/) documents how Backblaze also excludes `.git` folders and VeraCrypt encrypted drives. @AegirLeet: *"At some point, Backblaze just silently stopped backing up my encrypted (VeraCrypt) drives. Just stopped working without any announcement, warning or notification."* This is effectively a product downgrade disguised as a policy change.

**WordPress.org closed 31 plugins** after [a buyer spent six figures on Flippa acquiring 30+ plugins and planting backdoors](https://anchor.host/someone-bought-30-wordpress-plugins-and-planted-a-backdoor-in-all-of-them/). The malware was dormant for 8 months before activation. The backdoor used an Ethereum smart contract for command-and-control domain resolution — meaning traditional domain takedowns are ineffective. WordPress.org's forced update neutralized the phone-home mechanism but did *not* clean the injected code from wp-config.php.

**Google announced a new spam policy** for ["back button hijacking"](https://developers.google.com/search/blog/2026/04/back-button-hijacking) (858 points, 486 comments on Hacker News) — sites that manipulate browser history to trap users will face search penalties.

**Microsoft is renaming Copilot** in Windows 11, not removing it (369 points). The rebranding was initially reported as a removal, generating confusion.

**OpenAI's $852B valuation faces investor scrutiny** amid strategy shifts, [per Reuters/FT](https://www.reuters.com/legal/transactional/openai-investors-question-852-billion-valuation-strategy-shifts-ft-reports-2026-04-14/) (84 points).

**Takeaway**: The WordPress supply chain attack introduces a genuinely new threat vector — using blockchain for C2 infrastructure. If you manage WordPress sites, the forced plugin update alone is insufficient; you need to manually audit wp-config.php.

### What pricing and monetization models are worth studying?

**boringBar's real-time pricing pivot** is the most instructive case study today. @a-ve launched with a subscription, got immediate pushback from 296 commenters, and pivoted within hours to **$40 perpetual license for 2 devices + 2 years of updates**. After updates expire, you keep using the version you have. This is the "Sketch model" — buy once, get updates for a period, then optionally renew. It works because: (1) utility software has low ongoing costs, (2) users value certainty over optionality, (3) the renewal creates natural upgrade revenue without subscription anxiety.

**DaVinci Resolve's hardware-subsidized freemium** deserves study. As @raincole asked in the Hacker News discussion: *"How do they actually make money? I've been using Resolve for years without paying for it."* The answer: Blackmagic Design sells professional hardware (cameras, capture cards, control surfaces). The free software creates an enormous user base that funnels into hardware purchases. The new Photo feature extends this to photographers — a new hardware-buying demographic. This model only works if you have a physical product to sell, but it's the most generous freemium in professional software.

**The $228/month SaaS stack** from Reddit (@Capable_Document3744): RB2B ($99/mo) + Clay ($79/mo) + SalesRobot ($50/mo) = $228/month generating $22,000/month in pipeline. The lesson: don't build the platform, build the *workflow*. Each tool is cheap individually; the value is in the orchestration. This is a meta-pricing insight — your product's price should reflect its position in the customer's value chain, not its feature count.

**Reddit's @Dubinko** on SaaS pricing reality at $2K MRR: *"Half the features I spent weeks on, nobody uses. The dumb little thing I built in an afternoon? That's what people actually pay for."* This validates the "ship the minimum, charge for the obvious" approach.

**Takeaway**: The boringBar pivot pattern — launch subscription, measure resistance, pivot to perpetual within hours — should be a deliberate strategy, not a scramble. Pre-plan your pricing fallback before any public launch.
